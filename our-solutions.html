Thermoplastics for HPHT Applications

The oil and gas producers are continuously challenged due to the growing demand for hydrocarbons and as a result are constantly pushing to locate and produce new downhole reserves. This means that they encounter increasingly aggressive downhole conditions. One such demanding condition is High Pressure High Temperature (HPHT), herein the environment reduces the available range of materials to deploy in such deep reservoirs.

HPHT operations are often divided into the three tiers as shown below. Segment 2 (in yellow) usually involves wells with fluid pressures of up to 20,000 psi (138 MPa) and temperatures up to 400 °F (204°C). These wells either currently exist or are becoming increasingly common in the number of upcoming projects globally. The NA oil & gas market is actively involved in major projects with HPHT in the Gulf of Mexico and various land reserves. Oil companies are concentrating on solving major challenges they faced during the development and production of such fields which has their Leading equipment manufacturers and energy service companies further interested in new thermoplastic material developments to meet such challenges. There a few on-going selective exploration trials in the area of Segment 3 (in gold) that remain a challenge technically and economically offshore. No information is currently available for exploration activities in Segment 4 (in red).

Mitsubishi Chemical Advanced Materials understands that energy service companies operating equipment in the second and third tier are challenged on high pressure and high temperature (HPHT). That is why we offer our portfolio of solutions with a focus on improved chemical resistance, higher compressive strength, reduced frictional drag, reduced weight, and improving overall efficiency while increasing your MTBR (mean time between repairs). Below is an illustration to help you select a material for sealing or for selecting an anti-extrusion backup ring material.

The benefits of using materials from Mitsubishi Chemicals Advanced Materials are high mechanical strength, materials operating in temperatures up to 500°F (260°C), excellent dimensional stability and low coefficient of thermal expansion. Chemical and corrosion resistance is a functional component of HPHT, which is why MCAM continues to test and support the Oil & Gas materials for Norsok M-710 (sour gas aging) compliance. This is offered for our Ketron® PEEK stock shapes and machined components.

Mitsubishi Chemical Advanced Materials Research & Development team continues to develop solutions to meet the challenging needs of the design engineers where performance and reliability are required in extreme and hostile environments. Advanced properties of chemical and abrasion resistance combined with HPLT/ HPHT ratings and superior strength make the MCAM family of materials the ideal choice for structural parts, bushings, bearings, seals and back up rings.

The Effects of Corrosion on Labryinth Seals

Petroleum is divided into two types regarding sulfur content. Petroleum which contains high levels of sulfur is called sour crude oil. The opposite of sour crude oil is “Sweet crude oil” which contains small amounts of hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and carbon dioxide (CO2). The higher quality, low-sulfur, sweet crude oil is commonly used for processing into gasoline and is in high demand, particularly with heavy producers globally.

Inside of the past 10 years, exploration and production companies in both North America and Europe have developed newer technologies to extract fossil fuels in deeper elevations of the subsea floor. These formations are producing higher temperatures and pressures (HPHT) with elevated concentrations of hydrogen sulfide (H2S). Typical wells are producing fossil fluids with a highly corrosive and abrasive content indicating <10% of hydrogen sulfide. Mitsubishi Chemical Adavanced Materials, a leader in engineered thermoplastics for the energy market has been supporting this industry with engineered thermoplastics such as Ketron® Peek which has been tested to meet the conditons set forth by NORSOK M-710, in a sour multi-phase fluid mixture, but also in a single-phase gas test environment (with high H2S levels).

Mitsubishi Chemical Advanced Materials’ material solution for corrosives environments

Fluorosint® 500 – Used as an abradable seal, most commonly applied in “Tooth on Rotor” designs

Ketron® PEEK – Applied in Compressors with “Tooth on Stator” used in higher temperature, and those units with critical speeds

The lower seal is a PEEK labyrinth seal where the teeth deflect under contact or rub condition and return to the original position to continue operating and improving compressor efficiency with a restricted flow path.

Thermoplastic Labyrinth Seals

Labyrinth seals are commonly found in centrifugal compressors used to increase the gas pressure. Labyrinth seals manufactured from polymers are essentially a series of angled machined annular grooves acting as orifices (orifice allow flow to continue at new reduced pressure and slow the flow rate on the low pressure side) to reduce a region of high pressure from the low pressure region.

Labyrinth seals are not to be confused with labyrinth bushings which act as fluid isolator to keep high pressure or contaminants from a certain region. Labyrinth seals in a centrifugal compressor are actually “clearance seals” which leak by design. The engineer developing a labyrinth seal design needs to determine “how much clearance can be applied” without affecting the labyrinth seal grooves. The illustration below points out the locations in a centrifugal compressor where labyrinth seals are positioned.

Within centrifugal compressors labyrinth seals play a major role on the efficiency of the compressor. A centrifugal compressor function is to increase gas pressure and the pressure is contained by the various labyrinth seals positioned throughout the compressor that block high pressure from the low pressure that keeps the compressor operating smoothly. Unlike metal labyrinth seals which are designed with a larger ID clearance to eliminate contact during a rub condition, (radial) tighter clearances in metal labyrinth cannot be done without being impacted by rub condition, vibration on the rub condition, or heavy contact with the rotating element. Any contact with the metal labyrinth seal, whether minor or major, can negatively increase the flow path thereby reducing the compressor efficiency. From the impact of the rotating element, the annular grooves become rolled or mushroomed on the labyrinth. Below the illustration depicts how the metal labyrinth seal impacts compressor efficiency.

The illustration points out that increased labyrinth clearances and damages to the labyrinth seal, possibly bearings, and rotating element have significantly reduced compressor efficiency due to dynamic operational changes.

Now let’s review the same scenario but we will exchange the metal labyrinth seal with a thermoplastic (Ketron® or Duratron®) labyrinth seal.

First, we can start by adjusting annular groove and primary ID and lowering it by 50% including angling the grooves ~10 degrees minimum. The design engineer can alter the ID up or down dimensionally based on the operating conditions of their specific centrifugal compressor.

One key benefit is in a dynamic rub condition, the polymer teeth deflect on contact instead of the rounding/flattening found with the metal labyrinth seals. A second benefit is that there is no grooving or impact on the rotating element as the teeth return to original position and maintaining the machined clearance. The compressor operation is secured while increasing both the efficiency and reducing unnecessary and costly repairs.

You have now been exposed the issues commonly associated with the use metal labyrinth seals as well as the benefits of using thermoplastic labyrinth seals.
